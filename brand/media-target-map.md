# Cited media-target map for a proof-first authority on trustworthy AI

*Second, independent deep-research pass (2026-06-13). Converges with the earlier
`media-research.md` on the spine — gated/costly signals beat reach; Kindle "change how";
GitHub stars are noise — and upgrades the academics/policy/boards channels with more
specific, better-sourced venues. The `media:` block in `brand.yml` compresses the
highest-confidence recommendations here.*

## Executive verdict
The shortest path to authority is not broader reach. It is a stack of costly, inspectable
signals each audience already treats as real: artifact-audited methods outputs for
academics; concise evidence briefs + standards/workshop participation for policy experts;
peer-vouched board education + search-firm/advisory pathways for boards. Runnable tools with
external use beat raw visibility; retail-discovery channels like Amazon KDP are weak primary
authority-builders for these three audiences.

**Kindle verdict — change how, sharply.** A very large self-published technical/AI Kindle
catalog is unlikely to strengthen credibility with academics, policy experts, or boards.
Self-published books receive little academic weight (they bypass review); in the current
Amazon environment (AI-content disclosure rules + volume caps for abuse control), sheer
volume reads as "content farm," not expertise. Keep books only as few, flagship, heavily
sourced titles, each anchored to a runnable artifact, course adoption, or reproducible study.

## Audience reality
**Academics.** Discovery runs through scholarly search + preprint ecosystems (Google Scholar,
academic social networks, peer recommendation, library catalogues) — but discovery ≠ proof.
arXiv states its moderation is not peer review. The costly signals that survive: conventional
peer review, artifact audits/badges, preregistered/registered-report workflows. In a 122,000-
publication study, preprints carried ~20.2% average citation advantage and open data ~4.3%,
while code-sharing alone showed no significant advantage — openness helps, but the strongest
signal is paper + method + inspectable artifact, not "code exists somewhere."

**Policy experts.** Time-pressed; discover via internet search + traditional media (CIPR
study), but credibility/trustworthiness rated important by 99%; paywalls and missing
recommendations are barriers; high value placed on speaking with experts directly. A
public-health-policymaker survey: common sources were government/professional-body websites,
evidence summaries/review articles, and area experts. So: make evidence easy to scan,
problem-relevant, attributable, open-access, and easy to escalate into a conversation.
Evidence summaries/briefs beat full reviews; knowledge brokers/seminars improve uptake.

**Boards & executives.** Thinner public evidence, distinct ecosystem: boards discover/vet
through management reporting, peer-director communities, board education, and search/advisory
intermediaries — not open-internet popularity. NACD 2025: boards moving from AI awareness to
integrated governance. PwC / The Conference Board: barriers are limited capacity, slow
response to emerging risk, keeping pace with digital transformation; 99% of executives think
boards should use AI in oversight more. Channels: NACD chapter network (peer relationships),
NACD + Carnegie Mellon AI-oversight certificate, Corporate Board Member AI Leadership Forum,
Russell Reynolds board/tech-advisory search. Credible signal: "tools, scenarios, and
governance-ready judgment peers/educators/search pros will vouch for," not "he has ideas."

## Recommended channels (roles, fit, win conditions)
- **Artifact-audited methods venues (ACM artifact review + badges)** — proof; academics first.
  Win: badged paper, independent reproduction, citations referencing the artifact, outside
  institutions using the tool. Avoid: an "AI theory" paper without runnable code.
- **Registered Reports (where the question permits)** — proof; method reviewed before results
  (COS; >300 journals; RRs outperform on rigor). Win: Stage-1 acceptance → completed report.
- **GitHub + docs + live demo** — proof/discovery; external USE, not stars (NDSS: weak
  stars↔downloads; deployments correlate; stars gameable). Win: named adopters, reproducible
  install, external PRs/issues, package metrics, paper/policy citations.
- **arXiv + SSRN** — discovery only; rapid distribution, not final proof (arXiv: not peer
  review; SSRN: early-stage). Win: preprint → downstream audited publication / standards /
  brief.
- **Artifact-linked policy briefs on skepticism.ai** — voice/discovery; policy audiences
  search the web but reward concise, attributed, recommendation-bearing, demo-linked evidence.
  Win: staff/analyst citations, demo requests, testimony invitations.
- **NIST AI workshops + standards / AI Consortium** — proof + elite discovery; NIST convenes
  gov/academia/industry on trustworthy-AI measurement. Bring a benchmark/metric/tool. Win:
  workshop slot, comments adopted in drafts, consortium involvement, citation in guidance.
- **Stanford HAI / OECD-GPAI / selected convenings** — discovery + credibility transfer for
  tool-backed governance interventions. Win: invited talks, roundtables, commissioned memos,
  working-group roles. (Brookings/FAR.AI events = lower-confidence amplifiers.)
- **NACD + CMU-NACD board education** — discovery + credibility transfer; enter via teaching/
  scenarios, not "AI thought leadership." Win: teach a board workshop, repeat faculty,
  director referrals.
- **Search-firm + tech-advisory-board intermediaries (e.g. Russell Reynolds)** — discovery +
  vetting; the gatekeepers boards use. Win: shortlists, advisory-board seats, retained work.

## Ranked keep / cut
**Keep:** artifact-audited venues (highest academic proof) · GitHub with documented external
use · NIST standards/workshops (cross-audience) · NACD/CMU board education · artifact-linked
policy briefs on skepticism.ai · selected policy convenings (HAI/OECD, careful on Brookings/
FAR.AI) · arXiv/SSRN as a layer beneath stronger proof.
**Cut:** a large self-published Kindle catalog as a primary authority bet · GitHub
star-chasing · generic "AI commentator" social posting as a primary strategy (inference) ·
preprints without audited artifacts or venue conversion · board outreach built around public
repo links (inference).

## Source quality & limitations
Strongest, multi-source: ACM + COS (reproducibility/registered reports); CIPR, Campbell, NSW
evidence-use review (policy communication); NACD + PwC/The Conference Board + Russell Reynolds
(board ecosystems). Lower-confidence amplifiers (lean on org self-description): Corporate Board
Member programs, specific Brookings AI events, FAR.AI TIAP. The board and Kindle verdicts are
partly inference from channel mismatch + the current Amazon/AI-book environment; the academic
Kindle downside is better-evidenced than the board downside.
