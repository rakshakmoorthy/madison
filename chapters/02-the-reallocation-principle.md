# Chapter 2 — The Reallocation Principle
*The celebration happened before anyone asked what had actually been decided.*

A brand team finishes a sprint and the numbers look good. Twenty social variants, produced in one afternoon. The AI handled the copy, the format permutations, the headline options. Everyone agrees it was fast. Two days later the same team is still arguing: Who is the audience for this campaign? Which proof point should lead? Is the claim even legally cleared? Why does this campaign exist at this moment?

The execution accelerated. The decision quality did not move.

This is not a story about AI failing. The tool did exactly what it was asked. The problem sits one layer up, in the assumption that velocity is the same thing as progress. In advertising, those two things came apart long before AI existed. A team that can produce faster is not automatically a team that produces better. What changes with AI is the size of the gap — because now the acceleration is real, and the decision work is still entirely human, and if no one is deliberate about that divide, the gap widens until the artifacts are polished and the judgment is missing.

The Reallocation Principle is the answer to that gap. Not less AI. Better division of labor.

---

## What Work Actually Is

Before you can reallocate anything, you need a vocabulary for the different kinds of work a branding or advertising practitioner does in a week. Not all work is the same. Some of it is pattern work — the kind where you already know the shape of the output, and the task is mainly assembly: format this brief into a template, draft three headline variants on this proof point, extract the key claims from this research summary. That work has a defined structure. It can be described. It can be checked for completeness. An AI system can do a credible version of it.

But there is other work that has no pattern. Deciding whether a campaign idea is true to a brand's actual identity requires knowing things that aren't in any brief — the accumulated history of what the brand has claimed and whether those claims held up, the tacit understanding of how this particular client responds to risk, the judgment about whether this cultural moment is the right one or the wrong one. That work cannot be delegated. Not because the tool lacks capability in some narrow technical sense, but because the output of that work is not a file. It is accountability. Someone's professional judgment is attached to it. The decision is theirs.

Call these two categories execution and judgment, for short. Execution is the work that produces artifacts from known inputs according to known patterns. Judgment is the work that decides what the artifacts are for, whether they are good enough, and who is accountable when they move.

A third category sits between them: evidence. Evidence is the work of making claims inspectable — sourcing a data point, logging where an input came from, verifying that a generated summary matches its source. Evidence work is not exciting. It also does not get done unless someone is specifically responsible for it. AI can help gather; it cannot substitute for verification, because the point of evidence is not the content but the accountability behind it. Someone signed off that this is true.

Then there is approval. Approval is the gate. It is the moment when a practitioner says, in effect: I have seen what this artifact claims, I understand what it will do when it moves forward, and I accept responsibility for that. No AI participates in approval. The gate is a human act. This is not a technical limitation. It is the definition of what professional accountability means.

And finally there is learning. Learning is the work of extracting signal from outcomes — did this campaign claim perform? Was the audience hypothesis correct? What does a pattern across the last six months tell us about what works for this client? Learning is chronically under-resourced in agency and brand team work because execution pressure is relentless. But without learning, the team runs the same experiments and draws the same wrong conclusions on an accelerating treadmill.

Caption: The flow is linear per campaign but cyclical across campaigns; the learning gate feeds the next round of execution.] -->

Five categories. The Reallocation Principle is the claim that AI disrupts only one of them — execution — and that if a team treats the disruption as a reason to produce more artifacts without reinvesting in the other four, the team degrades its own professional value faster than the technology is building it.

## The Automation Candidate

Here is where it gets concrete. You have a week of planned brand work. You have time estimates for each task. The question is: for each item on the list, which of the five categories does it belong to?

This classification is not always obvious. Consider the task "draft the campaign brief." At first glance that sounds like execution — it is a document with a known structure, and AI can produce a credible draft of it. But the key decisions that go into a brief — the strategic claim, the audience prioritization, the reason-to-believe — are judgment. The brief is an execution artifact that encodes judgment decisions. Which means: AI can draft the container; humans must make the decisions that fill it. If the drafted brief ships without a human making those decisions explicitly, the brief looks complete and is professionally empty.

This is where teams go wrong most reliably. A tool that produces a well-formatted artifact creates the visual impression that the work is done. The format is complete. The judgment is missing. Nobody wrote "I decided this" anywhere in the document, because the document was generated and approved was replaced by accepted.

The test for whether a task is an automation candidate is this: if the AI produces the output and no human makes an explicit decision about it, what happens? If the answer is "nothing breaks visibly" — that is a warning sign, not clearance. What breaks invisibly is the chain of accountability. Two months later when a claim is challenged, nobody can say who decided it was the right claim. Nobody logged the verification step. The artifact exists; the professional judgment does not.

A properly classified task list tells you where your attention should go. Not where the AI cannot help — the AI can help with more than people use it for. But where the professional stakes live. The judgment tasks, the approval gates, and the learning synthesis are the places where a practitioner's expertise is non-substitutable. If those are being squeezed because execution now takes less time, the team is trading its professional core for throughput.

## The Reinvestment Problem

Suppose the reallocation works. AI handles execution. The team frees up three hours a week that used to go to drafting and formatting. The question is: where does that time go?

This is not rhetorical. Teams that implement automation without specifying reinvestment watch the freed time disappear into the ambient pressure of more work — more execution, faster cycles, higher volume. The output number goes up. The judgment quality stays flat or degrades because judgment requires sustained attention and that attention is now getting interrupted by the additional volume.

The reinvestment decision is the Reallocation Principle in practice. It requires naming, specifically, what decision is going to improve because AI is handling lower-level work. Not "we will have more time for strategy." That is a category, not a decision. The specific form is: "We are going to invest the time we recover from brief formatting into a weekly audience review, which will let us make an explicit decision about audience prioritization rather than inheriting the default from the previous campaign."

<!-- → [CHART: Before/after time allocation bar chart — two horizontal stacked bars, one labeled "Current Week" and one labeled "Reallocated Week" — segments for Execution, Evidence, Judgment, Approval, Learning — Current Week shows Execution at approximately 60%, other categories thin — Reallocated Week shows Execution compressed to approximately 30%, Learning and Judgment expanded — caption: The numbers are illustrative; the point is that reallocation without explicit reinvestment means the freed time fills with more execution, not better judgment.] -->

The reinvestment hypothesis is the testable claim at the center of the reallocation plan. It has a specific structure: "By moving X from human to AI-assisted, we free Y hours per week. Those hours will be reinvested in Z. The expected improvement is a better decision about W, measurable by V." Everything before the word "measurable" is strategy. Everything after it is how you find out if the strategy was right.

This matters because Madison treats generated artifacts as unverified by default. A reallocation plan that says "we expect better work" without naming the specific decision that improves is itself an unverified claim. The plan is only useful if it is inspectable — if a teammate who picks it up three months later can see what was tried, what was expected, and what happened.

## The Supervision Frame

There is a version of this chapter that talks about AI agents as tools and stops there. That framing is incomplete. The more precise framing is supervision.

When a practitioner uses an AI system to draft campaign materials, there is now an agent in the workflow. That agent has a scope — what it was asked to do — and an output — what it produced. Between those two things, the human's job is not consumption but supervision. Not "did the AI produce a thing?" but "did the AI produce the right thing, and do I understand what it did well enough to be accountable for it?"

Supervision has three questions.

Scope: what exactly is the agent allowed to do? This is not just a settings question. It is a professional clarity question. If the agent's scope includes drafting claims, the supervising practitioner needs to know whether those claims are verified before they move. If the scope includes audience inference, the supervising practitioner needs to know the inputs the inference was based on and whether those inputs are current.

Approval: who decides whether the output moves forward? This is the gate question. In a well-structured workflow, every AI-assisted output has a named human who evaluated it before it crossed into the next phase. Not "someone reviewed it" but "I reviewed it and I am accountable for what it says."

Verification: what evidence would make the output defensible? This is the evidence question. A generated claim is not evidence. A claim with a traceable source, a logged verification step, and a named reviewer is evidence. The distinction is not pedantic. When a campaign claim gets challenged — by a client, by legal, by a regulator, by the market — the question is not whether the claim is in a document. It is whether anyone checked it.

<!-- → [DIAGRAM: Three-node supervision frame — nodes labeled Scope, Approval, Verification — arranged as a triangle with arrows between each — central label "Accountable Output" — each node annotated with the question it answers (Scope: "What was the agent allowed to do?", Approval: "Who decides if this moves forward?", Verification: "What evidence supports this claim?"). Caption: The three questions are not sequential; they must all be answered before an AI-assisted output crosses a professional gate.] -->

The Madison posture — the operating framework this book is built around — uses the supervision frame as the minimum standard for any AI-assisted workflow. Execution work moves through AI assistance; judgment, approval, and evidence work stays explicitly human; the boundary between them is logged, not assumed.

This is not a conservative position on AI capability. It is a position on professional accountability. The question of what AI can do technically is separate from the question of what counts as a professional act. A professional act is one that carries the judgment and accountability of a named practitioner. AI cannot perform a professional act. It can assist in preparing for one.

## Building the Plan

The reallocation plan is a small document. It should be small enough to run and explicit enough to audit.

It has five columns: the task, the current time estimate, the category it belongs to, what AI assistance is appropriate, and what the human gate is. It has one more element: the reinvestment hypothesis.

The hypothesis is one sentence. "By automating [X], we free [Y hours] to reinvest in [Z], which should improve the decision about [W]."

If you cannot write the hypothesis, you do not have a plan. You have a list. The hypothesis is the thing that makes the list purposeful — it names the decision that is supposed to get better, which means you can check in a week whether it did.

Caption: The plan is a hypothesis, not a contract. Its value is in making the expected decision improvement explicit so the team can check whether it happened.] -->

One constraint worth keeping: no approval gate disappears in the plan. If execution gets faster, the approval work should get better — more thorough, more explicit, more traceable — not eliminated. A workflow in which automation speeds up execution and compresses approval is a workflow optimizing for volume at the expense of accountability. That trade degrades in the same direction as the social variant story at the start of this chapter: twenty artifacts, zero ownership, two days of argument.

The plan works when the judgment is better than it was. When the audience decision is more explicit. When the claim verification is more traceable. When the learning from this campaign actually feeds the next one instead of evaporating in the sprint pressure.

That is the Reallocation Principle. Not: use AI to make more things. Use AI to buy back attention for the work that cannot be delegated, so that the things you make are built on decisions someone actually owns.

## What Would Change My Mind

The strongest version of the counter-argument is that professional accountability in advertising is largely ceremonial — that approval gates exist for liability management, not quality, and that compressing them while producing more artifacts is a rational response to market pressure. If that is true, then reinvesting freed time into better judgment produces no measurable benefit, because the judgment was never really load-bearing.

I think this is wrong, but it is a testable disagreement. The reallocation plan is designed to surface it. If you run the plan, improve decision explicitness, and find no downstream improvement in campaign performance, client trust, or claim defensibility, the accountability hypothesis is weaker than I am arguing. Run the plan. Check the hypothesis. The evidence should decide.

## Still Puzzling

The hardest part of the classification task is hybrid work — tasks where execution and judgment are genuinely entangled. A seasoned strategist drafting a brief may be doing judgment work at the same time as execution work, not sequentially. The act of writing the document is part of how the decision gets made. Automating the drafting may not just accelerate execution; it may short-circuit the judgment process that happened through the writing. I do not have a clean answer to this. The supervision frame helps — but it does not fully resolve the question of what gets lost when drafting is no longer the thinking.

## LLM Exercises

**Exercise 1 — Task Classification**
Paste a week of your planned brand work into the chat. Ask the LLM to classify each task into one of five categories: execution, evidence, judgment, approval, learning. Then review the classification. Where did the LLM get it right? Where did it misclassify — and what does that tell you about which tasks are genuinely ambiguous?

**Exercise 2 — Automation Candidate Audit**
Take the execution tasks from your classified list. For each one, use the LLM to draft the output the task would normally produce. Then ask yourself: what decision is missing from this artifact? Write that decision down explicitly. Now you have identified the human gate.

**Exercise 3 — Reinvestment Hypothesis**
Ask the LLM to help you draft a reallocation plan in table form using the five-column structure in this chapter. Then write the reinvestment hypothesis yourself — no AI assistance. The hypothesis must name a specific decision and a specific way to check whether it improved. If you cannot write it, identify which piece of information you are missing.

**Application**

4. *(Moderate)* You are a brand strategist. Your team has just adopted an AI tool that can draft campaign briefs in about fifteen minutes, replacing a task that used to take two hours. Write the reinvestment hypothesis for this change. Name the specific decision that should improve, how much time it will receive, and how you will check in one week whether the decision quality improved. *What this tests: whether you can apply the hypothesis structure to a real scenario.*

5. *(Moderate)* A colleague argues: "We already have an approval process — legal reviews every claim before launch. So the accountability problem is solved." Using the three supervision questions from this chapter (scope, approval, verification), identify what the colleague's argument gets right and what it misses. *What this tests: whether you can use the supervision frame analytically rather than just descriptively.*

6. *(Moderate)* Take any recurring deliverable in your current work — a weekly report, a content calendar, a brief template. Classify the work it involves across the five categories. **Synthesis**

7. *(Challenging)* The chapter identifies a failure mode where automation speeds up execution and compresses approval — producing more artifacts with less accountability. Describe the conditions under which a team would drift into this failure mode without noticing. What would the early warning signs look like? 8. *(Challenging)* "Still Puzzling" raises the question of hybrid work — tasks where execution and judgment are entangled, such that automating the drafting may short-circuit the decision. Identify one task from your own professional context where this might be true. Describe what you think happens cognitively when you write that task by hand versus when you review an AI draft of it. Is there a way to preserve the judgment process while still benefiting from the automation? *What this tests: whether you can engage with a genuinely unresolved question in the framework rather than applying settled answers.*

**Challenge**

9. *(Open-ended)* The Reallocation Principle assumes that professional accountability in advertising is load-bearing — that better judgment decisions produce meaningfully better outcomes. The counter-argument is that approval gates are largely ceremonial, and that producing more artifacts faster is the rational response to market pressure regardless of decision quality. Design a test you could run over four weeks that would produce evidence bearing on this question. What would you measure? What result would support the Reallocation Principle? What result would challenge it? *What this tests: whether you can treat the chapter's central claim as a hypothesis rather than a conclusion, and reason about what evidence would actually move the question.*
