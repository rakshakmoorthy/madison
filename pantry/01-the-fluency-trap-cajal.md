# CAJAL Figure Report — The Fluency Trap
_Density: recommend 3 figures, Mixed._

This chapter carries three figurable concepts. First, the five claim types (verified, inferred, unsupported, taste judgment, approval-needed) are a taxonomy that organizes the entire audit — strong hierarchy/taxonomy candidate. Second, the "two worlds" thought experiment (the identical sentence under a sourced report versus an unsourced AI brief) is a clean comparison-panel candidate that dramatizes why fluency is not evidence. Third, the Claim Audit Table is the chapter's named primary output, with a defined seven-column structure — a structural schematic. The fluency-vs-competence point and the pattern-completion mechanism are prose-only and do not need figures. Density is moderate because the chapter is argumentative, not procedural.

## Figure 1 — The Five Claim Types
Priority: Critical · Type: hierarchy/taxonomy

BLOCK 1 — ILLUSTRAE PASTE BLOCK:
Create a flat vector taxonomy for a single-column textbook page showing five categories of claim that a reader can find inside any fluent artifact. Arrange the five categories as five equal horizontal rows or as five boxes in a clean vertical stack descending from least to most consequential. The five categories, in order, are: a verified claim that has a traceable source; an inferred claim that follows reasonably from evidence but is not directly measured; an unsupported claim stated as fact with no visible trail; a taste judgment that is evaluative rather than fact-checkable; and an approval-needed claim that requires authorization before it can move. Give each category a distinct reserved label area and a small distinguishing color band on its left edge so the five are visually separable. Reserve a slot beside each row where a one-line definition would later sit. Keep the categories visually parallel and evenly spaced, with generous white space, thin clean strokes, flat fills, and absolutely no baked-in words. The structure should read as a calm reference ladder, not a flowchart.

## Figure 2 — Two Worlds: Same Sentence, Different Status
Priority: Important · Type: comparison panels

In the left panel, depict a claim sitting at the top of a document with three supporting elements stacked beneath it connected by short vertical links — representing a survey citation, a competitor analysis, and a sales note — so the claim visibly rests on evidence. In the right panel, depict the same claim at the top of a document with nothing beneath it but empty space, then another equally smooth claim below it, also unsupported.
